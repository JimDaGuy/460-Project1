The Story Behind the Data

Seeing as our data is missing several countries, we can only speculate about the countries that have data associated with them.

The United States

From the start, it was fairly apparent that the United States seems to be the largest greenhouse gas emissions producer in our dataset. The U.S. consistently set the bar for the highest carbon dioxide(co2), hydrofluorocarbons(hcfs), nitrous oxide(n2o), and sulphur hexafluoride(sf6) emissions every year from 1990 to 2014. The only category the U.S. was not the lead producer of was methane(ch4), trailing closely behind Russia most years.

The Peaks

Listed below are the years and ammounts of peak emissions for each category of greenhouse gas in our data. As expected, the United States holds all of them except for methane. While the U.S. leads in all other categories, it should be noted that methane is roughly 30 times more potent than carbon dioxide, contributing much more to global warming (Source).

  • Carbon Dioxide (co2) - United States - 2005 - 6,122,746 kilotons
  • Hydrofluorocarbons (hcfs) - United States - 2014 - 157,237 kilotons
  • Methane (ch4) - Russia - 1990 - 942,383 kilotons
  • Nitrous Oxide (n2o) - United States - 1998 - 433,872 kilotons
  • Sulphur Hexafluoride (sf6) - United States - 1990 - 31,080 kilotons
  • Greenhouse Gasses (ghgs) - United States - 2007 - 7,422,207 kilotons

Emissions Disparity

While the United States and Russia are large contributors to global emissions of greenhouse gasses, the data shows that in all the other countries displayed, greenhouse gas emissions rarely if ever rise above half of Russia's emissions or even a fifth of the United States' emissions. This is likely in part due to the disparity in their populations but the difference is still staggering.
